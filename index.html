DESIGN AND PERFORMANCE ANALYSIS OF MIMO ANTENNA OPERATING AT 2.4GHz

Abstract

This project tackles the design ,simulation and fabrication of Multiple Input, Multiple Output (MIMO) antenna. This MIMO antenna utilizes two rectangular patches with curved corners, along with a special structure called a Defective Ground Structure (DGS) and with parasitic elements, all situated on a substrate made of FR4 epoxy with a specific electrical property (permittivity of 4.4). The goal is for this antenna to operate at a frequency of 2.4 GHz. A crucial aspect of MIMO antennas is isolation, which refers to how well the individual antenna elements within the MIMO system avoid interfering with each other's signals. This project aims to achieve an isolation level of at least 10 decibels (dB) at the desired operating frequency. Additionally, the project strives to maintain a similar radiation pattern and gain (the antenna's ability to focus its signal) compared to a MIMO antenna design without the isolation features. To achieve these objectives, the project will employ Ansys HFSS, a powerful software tool for antenna design and simulation. In essence, this project investigates a MIMO antenna design that offers good isolation while aiming to preserve the overall performance characteristics of a non-isolated design.

Simulation of Patch Antenna for NaVIC

Abstract

In this project, we present the design and development of a compact and efficient patch antenna tailored to operate in the L5 frequency range. The antenna is intended to be used in receivers and devices that require precise positioning capabilities, such as geolocation services, surveying, mapping, and precision timing applications. The geometry of the patch is carefully engineered to achieve broadside radiation patterns and minimize losses due to surface waves and radiation spillover. The antenna's resonant frequency is carefully tuned to match the L5 band's center frequency (1176.45 MHz), ensuring maximum gain and sensitivity at the desired frequency. The use of high-quality dielectric materials and manufacturing techniques ensures minimal signal losses and consistent performance across the entire L5 band. Simulated results and measurements demonstrate the antenna's remarkable performance in terms of gain, and radiation patterns at the L5 band. These results validate the suitability of the patch antenna for NAVIC/IRNSS L5 band applications, showcasing its potential to enhance the accuracy and reliability of satellite-based navigation and positioning systems. Overall, the developed patch antenna serves as a promising solution to meet the stringent requirements of satellite navigation systems operating in the L5 band. Its exceptional performance characteristics make it a valuable candidate for various geospatial and positioning applications, contributing to the advancement of the satellite navigation field.
